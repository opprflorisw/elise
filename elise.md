## 1. What the studio actually is

Elise is **not** an interior decorator, **not** a classic architect, and **not** a property
developer. The studio sits precisely in the space *between* those disciplines:

**Interior Architecture & Property Transformation**
(NL: *architectonische woningtransformatie*)

The distinction matters enormously. A decorator usually arrives once the shell and the layout
are already fixed. Elise starts one level earlier — with structural, spatial questions:

- Should that wall even stay?
- Can we add a void / double-height space (vide) here?
- Can we rotate the living space?
- Would an extension make more sense here?
- Can daylight be organised differently?
- How does a person move through the home?
- How does the space *feel* at 08:00, 15:00 and 22:00?
- How do you create calm in an urban home?

This is much closer to architecture than to interiors.

### Candidate positioning names (to decide later)
Elise likes the sound of these — leaning away from "verbouwstudio / interieurbureau",
which feel too small:
- **Interior Architecture Studio** / Interior Architectural Studio
- **Residential Transformation Studio**
- Spatial Design Studio
- Urban Residential Studio
- "Design agency" — Elise said this *also sounds cool* ("vet")

The work is really about **architecture, space, material and atmosphere as one whole.**

## 3. Style & design DNA  *(this is the part Elise refined most — read carefully)*

### Important correction
Early in the conversation the style was summarised as *"ingetogen luxe / rust / minimalisme"*
(understated luxury, calm, minimalism). **Elise pushed back on this.** That framing is wrong.

Elise's own words:
> "Ingetogen luxe albeit niet zijn. Ik hou van bijzonder en eye catchers. Rust ja en nee. Het
> zal een luxe eclectic style zijn. Een mengelmoes van verschillende stylen en jaren/decennia.
> Denk aan Parisian appartementen en overdadige kleuren en materialen. Maar altijd chique en
> tijdloos. Maar zeker niet saai."

So: **not** understated minimalism. She loves the **special, the eye-catcher, daring**. Calm
"yes and no". A **luxe eclectic** signature — a mix of styles, eras and decades. Parisian-
apartment richness, abundant colours and materials — but **always chic and timeless, and
definitely never boring.**

### Refined again by her reference photos
After she shared images, the read got sharper. It's **not** ornament-heavy, print-heavy,
object-cluttered classic eclecticism. It's more:

- architectural
- sculptural
- gallery-like
- European metropolitan
- a mix of the 1930s, 1970s and contemporary design
- luxury materials **without** classic luxury symbols

Somewhere between **Paris, Milan, New York and Copenhagen** — but with more personality and
more daring. Explicitly *away* from Scandinavian / Japandi / beige minimalism.

### The five things visible in almost every reference image
1. **Sculptural furniture** — furniture isn't filler, it's the architecture of the room.
   Thick volumes, rounded forms, strong silhouettes, pieces that are almost artworks
   (blue sofa, Mario Bellini–like elements, chrome chairs, round volumes).
2. **Architecture first, interior second** — the interiors would still work with all furniture
   removed, because the architecture itself is strong: arches, panelling (lambrisering),
   sightlines, material transitions, special door openings, height play, strong proportions.
3. **Materials with depth** — walnut, travertine, natural stone, polished steel, chrome,
   high-grade leather, mohair, bouclé, lime paint (kalkverf), marble. But **never all at once** —
   always a controlled tension.
4. **Colour as a statement** — not neutral, not maximalist. Colour usually lands in one
   element: one sofa, one artwork, one stone, one wall, one object. Powerful, not busy.
5. **The 1970s are everywhere** — not retro, not vintage, but 70s elegance: chrome, tubular
   frames, round forms, rich fabrics, smoked glass, dark woods — combined with contemporary
   architecture.

### Style labels that fit
- Architectural Eclecticism
- Sculptural Luxury
- Contemporary European Modernism
- Curated Urban Luxury
- Collectible Design Living
- **Architectural Collectible Living** *(fits best — describes it even though it isn't a real movement)*

### The three design layers
- **Architectural layer:** strong layouts, sightlines, daylight, custom work, special spatial
  interventions, classic elements combined with modern architecture.
- **Material layer:** natural stone, wood, brass, steel, rich fabrics, gloss next to matte,
  old next to new.
- **Emotional layer:** surprise, drama, warmth, personality, collected objects, art, stories.

### What future clients should say
Not *"what a beautiful interior."* Instead:
- "This home feels like it could be in Paris or Milan."
- "I've never seen a home like this in Amsterdam."
- "This house could only have belonged to these residents."

## 4. Target audience

Deliberately narrow at the start. Not "everyone who's renovating." Rather:

**Urban homes roughly €500,000 – €2 million in Amsterdam, Den Haag and Rotterdam.**

Typical client:
- busy professionals
- dual-income couples
- entrepreneurs
- expats
- young families

They have taste, value quality, lack time, and are willing to invest when the result is right.

The brief is usually **not** "make my living room nice" but:
> "We bought a special property and want to make it something exceptional."

Not always extremely expensive — but the client must value design quality and character.

Property types: townhouses (herenhuizen), upper-floor apartments (bovenwoningen), lofts,
penthouses, urban family homes.

## 5. Services (simplify aggressively)

Eventually maybe just **two pillars**:

### 1. Residential Transformation
From first idea to delivery:
- spatial vision
- layout design
- material strategy
- lighting plan
- build supervision (verbouwbegeleiding)
- furnishing

### 2. Development Advisory
For developers, investors, hospitality. **Second pillar, not the main focus.**

### Hospitality — as inspiration, not a separate service
Don't position hospitality on its own. It's the **inspiration source**: the layering of a
boutique hotel, the lighting of a good restaurant, the material choices of a high-end lobby —
applied to *living*. That overlap (boutique hotels, private members' clubs, special
restaurants, luxury residences) is exactly where Elise's taste and high-end hospitality meet.

## 6. Positioning: the core insight

Elise is closer to a **boutique design studio** than a traditional interior firm — a
fundamentally different business to build.

The biggest challenge is **not technical**. It's not positioning herself too small as
"someone who's good at renovating," when she's actually building a **design vision** that sits
between living, architecture and property development. That's a rarer position than she may
yet realise.

The Netherlands has many studios doing Scandinavian, calm, beige minimalism. There's a market
for that. But there's growing demand for homes that feel personal, characterful, international,
cosmopolitan — the kind you see in Amsterdam, London, Paris. That's where Elise is distinctive.
